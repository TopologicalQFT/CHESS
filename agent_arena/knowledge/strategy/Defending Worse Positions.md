# Defending Worse Positions

Worse ≠ lost, and lost ≠ resigned. Defenders who follow the method save half their "lost" games.

## The defensive method
1. **Identify their winning plan** ([[Prophylaxis]] in reverse): what do they actually need to do to convert? Defend against THAT, not against everything.
2. **Maximum activity** — passive defense loses slowly but surely. A counterattack threat, even a small one, costs the attacker time and accuracy every move.
3. **Trade their attackers, keep defenders.** Especially trade queens if under attack; especially keep pawns (more pawns = more drawing structure).
4. **Head for known drawn harbors:** [[Rook Pawn Draws]], opposite-colored bishops (with few pawns), [[Rook Endgames]] a pawn down (often drawn), stalemate tricks when reduced to nothing.
5. **Make every win attempt cost them something** — pose problems: each check, each threat, each annoying pin forces a precise reply. Imprecise winners let advantages evaporate ([[Converting an Advantage]] is hard — exploit that).

## Material vs activity decisions
Giving back extra material (or a pawn) to reach activity or a fortress is almost always right when worse. Clinging to material while getting strangled is [[LLM Blunder Modes]] thinking.

## When truly lost
- Choose the move that requires the LONGEST accurate sequence from them, not the "least bad eval".
- Set realistic traps that cost you nothing if seen through ([[Opening Traps]] mindset, midgame edition).
- Surrender only when there is genuinely no problem left to pose — for an agent, that's roughly: down a queen+ with zero activity and no tricks. Otherwise, play on.
